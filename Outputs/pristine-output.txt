These systems *deliberately* architect final authority outside the statistical model. The prompt’s regime is compatible with, and in some cases *enhances*, such architectures by giving the human (or the watchdog model) a transparent, checkable rationale rather than a bare recommendation.

--------------------------------------------------------------------
1. *Advisory-only* medical triage

The absence of any claim about *where* final authority should reside is a feature, not a gap—it keeps the methodology usable whether the governance model is “human always decides,” “model decides unless confidence < X,” or “model proposes ranked list, human picks.”

--------------------------------------------------------------------
1. DO-178C Traceability  
   – Original guidance (1980s) “recommended” bidirectional trace requirements ↔ tests; avionics vendors treated it as optional until DERs made it *de-facto* mandatory for certification. No new regulation was passed; the *structural need* for auditability did the work.

2. PCI-DSS Requirement 10  
   – Early guideline (“maintain secure audit logs”) was non-prescriptive; once QSAs began failing merchants who could not produce *reconstructible* transaction chains, the optional became compulsory.

3. SOC-2 “points of focus”  
   – AICPA guidelines use permissive language (“may consider”); nevertheless, auditors issue qualified opinions when those points are absent, so vendors embed them in product roadmaps.

Pattern: when a regulator, insurer, or large customer needs to *verify* logic, an apparently “optional” documentation regime converges to *mandatory* once the surrounding tooling (storage cost, search latency, compliance templates) drops below an economic threshold.

Why the prompt’s *lack* of assertiveness is structurally intentional, not ineffective
--------------------------------------------------------------------
The prompt is not a product spec; it is a *meta-protocol*. By refusing to dictate output schema, it remains compatible with:
